🔬 Cellular Adaptation Overview

Cells adapt to changes in their environment through several mechanisms. Understanding these adaptations is crucial for recognizing pathological processes.

Atrophy

Decrease in cell size and function

  • Can occur due to disuse, poor nutrition, loss of nerve supply
  • Prevention: Active range of motion, exercises, proper nutrition
  • Example: Muscle wasting during prolonged bed rest

Hypertrophy

Increase in cell size

  • Example: Enlarged heart muscle in athletes vs. heart failure
Remember: Atrophy = A-trophy = Away/shrinking (cells get smaller). Hypertrophy = Hyper = More (cells get bigger).

Hyperplasia

Increase in the number of cells (vs. hypertrophy which is increase in cell size)

  • Example: Thyroid in persistent iodine deficiency
A patient on prolonged bed rest will likely experience atrophy. Nursing interventions include active range of motion exercises and proper nutrition to prevent muscle wasting.

🔄 Metaplasia

Definition: Reversible change in which one cell type changes to another — a protective response to chronic irritation.

Common Examples:

  • Smoking → Chronic exposure to toxins → Cells change from columnar to squamous epithelium
  • GERD → Chronic stomach acid irritates esophagus → Normal squamous epithelium replaced by columnar epithelium = Barrett's esophagus
Metaplasia is reversible if the irritant is removed! Think of it as the body putting on a "tougher coat" to protect itself.

⚠️ Dysplasia

Definition: Abnormal cell growth and organization within a specific tissue

  • Potential precursor to cancer
  • Often results from chronic inflammation
  • Example: Cervical dysplasia on Pap smear

The Progression Pathway:

Chronic Inflammation → Metaplasia → Dysplasia → Carcinoma
This progression pathway is why cervical cancer screening (Pap smears) is critical — it catches dysplasia before it progresses to carcinoma.

💀 Apoptosis

Definition: Programmed cell death — a regulated process that eliminates damaged or unnecessary cells.

Key Point: Failure of apoptosis can lead to neoplasia/cancer.

Think of apoptosis as the body's quality control — removing defective cells before they cause problems. When this system fails, uncontrolled growth (cancer) can result.

🔥 Cardinal Signs of Inflammation

  1. Redness (Rubor)
  2. Heat (Calor)
  3. Swelling (Tumor)
  4. Pain (Dolor)
  5. Loss of Function (Functio Laesa)

🛡️ Inflammatory Response

Chemotaxis

Promotes leukocyte migration to injury site — begins the cleanup process.

Histamine Release

Primary source: Mast cells

  • Promotes blood flow
  • Increases vessel permeability
Understanding the inflammatory response helps explain why injured areas become red, warm, and swollen — histamine from mast cells increases blood flow and vessel permeability.

🩹 Stages of Wound Healing

1. Hemostasis (Clotting) ▼

The body's emergency response at the beginning of wound healing.

  • Vessels narrow to reduce blood flow
  • A clot starts to form
2. Inflammation ▼

Occurs right after hemostasis and lasts a few days.

  • The body's cleanup stage
  • White blood cells fight infections
3. Proliferation ▼

Rebuilding of new tissue over a few weeks.

  • Fresh, red layer of skin called granulation tissue forms
4. Remodeling ▼

Final healing stage — can take up to a year.

  • Wound strengthens
  • Scar develops

🧵 Types of Healing

Primary Intention

Clean wound edges brought together → Minimal scarring

Secondary Intention

Wound can't be closed with stitches/staples → Edges can't be brought together → Heals from bottom up → Larger scars

🚨 Wound Complications

Wound Dehiscence

Disruption/separation of surgical incision or wound

Wound Evisceration

Protrusion of wound contents — a serious surgical complication

Pressure Ulcer Treatment

  • Debridement: Removing dead tissue to allow healthy tissue growth
  • Prevention: Shift frequently to reduce pressure on one spot
  • Eschar: Dead tissue that needs removal

🧬 Cystic Fibrosis

  • Characteristic: Thick, sticky mucus
  • Most common in Caucasians who are Ashkenazi Jews
  • Both parents must be carriers → 1 in 4 chance child has disease
  • Results from lack of a protein → imbalanced chloride

Treatments:

  • Chest Physiotherapy & Airway Clearance — performed 1 hour before meals
  • Corticosteroid Inhalers — decrease lung inflammation
  • Bronchodilators — widen airways, increase airflow
  • Acetylcysteine — mucolytic (patient may complain about odor)

🧬 Tay-Sachs Disease

  • Rare, inherited disorder — fatty substance builds up in the brain
  • Destroys nerve cells
  • Age of onset: ~6 months
  • Missing enzyme: Hex-A
  • Symptoms: Vision loss, difficulty swallowing, paralysis
  • Prognosis: Fatal (~5 years old)

🧬 Huntington's Disease

  • Autosomal dominant — one parent with HD = 50% chance
  • Adult onset: typically 30s-40s

Early Signs:

  • Chorea — jerky, random, uncontrollable movements
  • Behavior Changes — mood swings, depression, irritability

Progression:

  • Chorea increases, cognitive decline, motor control worsens
  • Difficulty in speech and swallowing

🧬 Down Syndrome

  • Caused by extra copy of chromosome 21 (Trisomy 21)
  • Affects ~1 in 800-1000 newborns
  • Diagnostic Test: Karyotyping
  • Features: Flattened face, short nose bridge, smaller ears, shorter neck, decreased muscle tone, developmental delays

🦠 Clostridium botulinum

  • Produces toxin preventing muscle contraction
  • ACh is NOT released → neural impulse not transmitted → flaccid paralysis

🦠 Clostridium tetani

  • Causes increased muscle tone
  • Affects Masseter muscles → "lockjaw"
  • Results in generalized muscle spasms
Botulinum = Floppy (flaccid paralysis). Tetani = Tight (muscle spasms/lockjaw). Opposite effects!

🦠 Staphylococcus aureus

Source of antibiotic-resistant strains:

  • MRSA — Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus
  • VRSA — Vancomycin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus

🧠 Bacterial Meningitis

Inflammation of the meninges (protective membranes covering brain and spinal cord)

  • Requires immediate medical attention
  • First-line treatment: Antibiotics

Most Common Pathogens:

  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Neisseria meningitidis
  • Haemophilus influenzae

Classic Clinical Presentation:

  • Neck stiffness (meningeal signs)
  • Altered mental status
  • Severe headache
  • Kernig's/Brudzinski's signs positive

Diagnostic Tests:

  • Lumbar puncture (Gold standard)
  • CSF analysis: ↑ Protein, ↓ Glucose, ↑ WBC, Positive culture

🦠 Epstein-Barr Virus

Causes infectious mononucleosis (mono)

  • Common in young adults
  • Symptoms: Fatigue, sore throat, lymph node swelling

🌡️ Pyrogens & Expectorants

Pyrogens: Substances that induce fever — signal the brain to increase body temperature to fight infection.

Expectorant: Medications that loosen and expel mucus from the respiratory tract, aiding in relieving bronchitis symptoms.
